What it is

The NetDispenser is a tool for parents and teachers, alike. Kids earn their internet access through a kiosk-style system by completing web activities in exchange for special router credits. Kids learn that the fastest way to their goal is to make a strong effort. Benefits include accountability and increased effectiveness of activities.

Why it exists

It was a product of necessity. I wanted my kids to read certain articles and without drama. Instead of me asking them to read, suddenly they were asking me for more things to read, i.e. more ways to earn time online. Moreover, the source of their motivation did not seem to diminish their learning or retention. Rather, it was merely the fact that they were motivated at all which made it effective. That means that any activity can be made more effective when assigned this way.

How it works

The NetDispenser is two pieces: A Credit-Meter, which is a Raspberry-Pi configured as a WiFi access point, broadcasts the Credit-Meter website locally. This device serves as the WiFi access point for the kids. It only gives them internet access when the Credit-Meter application is actively consuming the credits they have earned. When the credits are gone then the child must visit the Credit-Feeder website and perform more credit-earning activities which a parent or teacher has queued for them. Once earned, the credits are transferred back to the Credit-Meter with the push of a single button. The Credit-Feeder website is remote so that a parent can assign activities from the comfort of a remote office and then rest assured that the kids are working diligently.

Roadmap and Big Picture

This is a grass-roots project. I, Charles Cossé, began working on this in 2006. Since then it has been re-developed several times with upgrades hardware and software. The project isn't trying to sell anything or to make money for any one person. Rather, it seeks to provide two things that I wish I had when my own kids were younger: first, a tool to help parents supplement their childrens' education. Second, to make development of free education software a viable pastime, and attractive beyond mere altruism or showcasing of ones' abilities.

The mere mention of money raises eyebrows. My solution to aleviate concerns is to require a modest subscription fee to users who wish to utilize the platform, but to enable the subsriber to allocate 100% of their fee to the developer(s) of their choice.

To remain copacetic with the principles of openness and software freedom, we encourage opensource licenses and app design which can be used stand-alone and outside of the NetDispenser platform, i.e. no vendor lock-in tricks. Thus, any user can benefit from the included applications, because the apps are free software, and any developer can take their work wherever they like, because their work is only tied to the NetDispenser platform through a minimal, non-intrusive code block.

It is hoped that this arrangement can keep users and developers in closer contact and provide incentive for people to develop their ideas for new education software. Please join us!
